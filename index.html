Money apps split the bill. None of them split the work — so I built one that tracks both, in one fairness score.

Entry 01

The problem

Expense-splitting apps keep money fair in shared households. Nothing mainstream keeps labor fair — even though the imbalance is real, common, and well documented, not just a hunch.

63%of women report doing more than their fair share of household labor
22%of men report the same — despite most people believing chores should be split evenly

That imbalance is linked to stress, burnout, and strain on relationships and careers — and it shows up in any shared living situation, not just families. Meanwhile, the market leader in this space (Splitwise) has explicitly chosen to stay money-only.

Problem statement: shared-living partners have no single, trustworthy way to see if money and labor are actually being split fairly — so the imbalance stays invisible until it becomes resentment.

Entry 02

Who this is for

Two personas, both already managing shared expenses, neither with a way to track the other half of fairness.

Cohabiting partner · works full-time

Wants visibility, not nagging

Feels she's doing more of the invisible "remembering what needs doing" work, not just the visible tasks. A chore chart that feels like punishment would backfire — she wants the imbalance to be obvious without her having to point it out herself.

Shares a 2–3 person apartment

Already splits money, not labor

Uses Splitwise for rent and groceries already. Chore disputes happen in the group chat and get forgotten by the next morning. Wants something lightweight — not a system that feels like it's grading him.

Entry 03

The competitive landscape

Splitwise isn't a competitor to out-build at its own game. It's a category to extend.

Splitwise does wellWhere the gap is
Flexible expense splittingZero chore or labor tracking
Fast, clean settle-up flowNo combined "fairness" view
Large, loyal user baseRecently tightened free tier, frustrating long-time users
Deliberately avoids approval featuresNo way to surface unequal effort, only unequal $
Entry 04

Prioritization

Scored the feature backlog with RICE (Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort). Click a column to sort.

Feature Reach↕ Impact↕ Conf.↕ Effort↕ RICE↕

The interesting tension: the combined fairness score — the entire reason this product exists rather than being "Splitwise with a chore list" — scores below basic expense tracking, because its logic is genuinely harder to validate. A purely mechanical read of RICE would ship a slightly-better Splitwise clone. I overrode the ranking and kept a simplified fairness score in the MVP anyway, because it's the value proposition, not just another feature.

What I cut, and why

Manual chore weighting

+

Adds setup friction before users see any value. Flat weighting ships in v1; revisit once there's usage data on whether granular control is actually wanted.

Reminder / nudge notifications

+

High value, but a retention lever — not a proof-of-concept lever. Belongs in a v2 focused on keeping active users engaged, not validating the core loop.

"Swap a chore" negotiation

+

Interesting, but speculative. Not worth building until the core fairness loop is validated with real users.

Photo proof of completion

+

Lowest RICE score by a wide margin, and adds trust/privacy complexity disproportionate to its benefit.

Entry 05

User stories — MVP scope

AS A HOUSEHOLD MEMBER

I want to log a shared expense and split it by amount or percentage, so money owed is always clear.

AC: add expense → choose split method → balances update instantly.
AS A HOUSEHOLD MEMBER

I want to add and assign a recurring chore, so responsibilities are visible instead of assumed.

AC: create chore → set frequency → assign owner → mark complete.
AS A HOUSEHOLD MEMBER

I want to see one fairness view combining money and chores, so I can tell at a glance if the load is balanced.

AC: dashboard shows combined score, updates with new data.
AS A HOUSEHOLD MEMBER

I want to settle a money balance in one tap, so I don't have to do manual math.

AC: select "Settle Up" → see amount owed → confirm.

Entry 07

QA: what I caught

Catching my own product's flaws felt like part of acting as PO here, not a separate design-review step. In a fairness app specifically, a math error undermines the entire premise.

56Alex
45Sam
56 + 45 = 101 — doesn't add up

Each side was rounded independently. The fix: calculate one score directly, then derive the other as 100 − score, instead of rounding both separately.

Entry 08

How I'd measure success

ACTIVATION

Dual logging in week 1

% of new households that log ≥1 expense and ≥1 chore in the first 7 days — proves both halves of the value prop get used.

RETENTION

Week 4 vs week 1

Weekly active households, to see if the habit sticks past novelty.

CORE VALUE

Fairness Breakdown views

% of users viewing it more than once a week — signals the differentiator is actually being used, not just expense tracking.

TRUST

"Did this feel accurate?"

Post-onboarding survey question — trust in the scoring logic is the product's biggest risk.

Entry 09

What's next

  • Manual chore weighting — once data shows flat weighting actually frustrates users
  • Notification and reminder system, for retention
  • "Swap a chore" negotiation flow, once the core loop is validated
The most useful part of this project wasn't the screens — it was the moments where the framework and the judgment disagreed, and learning to explicitly justify overriding the data when strategy calls for it.
